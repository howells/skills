# Name Strategies

Use multiple strategies when generating names. A strong shortlist should not feel like the same name with different suffixes.

## Strategies

### Verb

A direct action name. Works well for tools and workflows when the product has a clear job.

Examples of shape: Gather, Forge, Capture, Compose, Dispatch.

### Metaphor

A concrete image that carries the product's behavior or promise. Works when the product needs memorability without explaining every feature.

Examples of shape: Atlas for navigation, Loom for weaving, Beacon for guidance.

### Compound

Two plain words joined into a memorable concept. Works when clarity matters more than mystery.

Examples of shape: Typewell, Fieldnote, Stackline.

### Portmanteau

Two ideas compressed into one coined term. Works when the name should be ownable, but it can become awkward if pronunciation is unclear.

Examples of shape: Notion, Vercel, Zapier.

### Short Word

A compact real or near-real word. Works when the product needs confidence, speed, and easy recall.

Prefer names with strong consonants and simple spelling.

### Prefix Or Suffix

A base word with a small modification. Useful for category signal, but easy to overuse.

Avoid tired suffixes unless they are strategically useful: `-ify`, `-ly`, `-base`, `-hub`, `-stack`, `-kit`.

### Domain Reference

A term from the user's field. Works when audience familiarity is valuable.

Use carefully: insider terms can be powerful for expert products and alienating for broad consumer products.

### Sound And Rhythm

A name chosen for mouthfeel, cadence, and memorability. Useful for brands that need to be said aloud.

Check that spelling remains obvious after hearing it.

## Validation Caveats

Availability checks are signals, not guarantees.

- Web search can miss private companies, newly launched products, and regional conflicts.
- Domain checks can vary by registrar and WHOIS privacy.
- DNS records do not prove a domain is purchasable.
- Package registry results can change at any time.
- Trademark clearance requires jurisdiction-specific legal review.

Use precise language: "no obvious conflict found" is usually more honest than "available".
